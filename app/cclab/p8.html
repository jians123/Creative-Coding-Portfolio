Project #8 Experimental Camera

Link to Code Sketch

Description

In the selfie camera I designed, three filters with visual effects are created through coding. During the coding process, I was also engaged in an intense collision of thoughts, as well as the perception of artistic senses. In the era of epidemic, modern people are living in a more socially-networked way. Therefore, photos are a main carrier to convey human emotions and sense of belonging. In a sense, filters can condense one’s expectations to reflect a variety of internal worlds. Different from the usual filters, users will find three special filters when they are using the unconventional camera. Users can click the start button in the upper left corner, move the mouse right to canvas and then left click, and use keyboard 1, 2, or 3 to switch between filters. Each of the three filters has its own source of inspiration.

Design Process

The first pixel filter is inspired by the Minecraft sandbox game. In three-dimensional space, players are free to create and destroy different kinds of cubes. Therefore, the filter is composed of retro pixels, allowing the user to roam in the world of imagination. Then, the main tone of the second filter is dark blue. The filter will add a more wonderful sense of rhythm to dynamic shots than still photos. My design was inspired by Van Gogh’s “Starry Night”, with its violet and blue hues, full of movement and changing starry sky. Finally, the third filter will distort portraits and objects, borrowing from the distorting mirror of childhood. In the world of Distorting Mirror, everything is spinning, and the circles seem to carry magic. The filter is covered with the innocence of children, but also tinged with the colors of adult worlds, seeming to be capable of illuminating the deepest thoughts in human heart. Each of the three filters has its own characteristics, therefore I applied all of them in the camera.

Reflection

In designing the unconventional camera, I rethought the societal aesthetics from my personal aesthetics to the general public’s. When filters with beautifying effects have become a social necessity, are we users embracing a new freedom or bound by the software that makes it easy for us to achieve a “pretty face”? Perhaps it is the beauty filter that chooses the aesthetics for us, rather than the other way around. In the post-epidemic era, virtual socialization is becoming the new norm. We need to shake off the shackle of aesthetic homogenization, embrace individual diversity, and accept our own imperfections. Therefore, in designing the unconventional camera, I appreciate the bold imagination and originality I put in coding them. The filter, as a rare self-expression, is a way to review the past and look into the future. Whether to choose a filter for life, it is up to every one of us to decide.
